# Inspector

The rape fields west of Rassvet were at full flower — that blunt, chemical yellow stretching all the way to the horizon, making the sky look washed-out and larger than usual — and Alexei Nikolaevich Gorlov, who had been district agricultural accounts officer in Yablonovka for twenty-one years, sat in the second car of the slow train with a folder of signed ledger printouts on his knees, adding the quarter-four figures in his head for the third time. The totals agreed as they had the first two times. He continued.

He had filed the quarterly audit with Marchuk that morning. Marchuk had taken the folder, set it on the pile, and said the formatting would need to come into line with the new regional standard by the third quarter submission. He did not specify which element. He handed Alexei a two-page guidance document. It was the same document Alexei had received in January.

There was a photograph of his son Pavel in the inside pocket of his briefcase — Pavel at seventeen at the district agricultural fair, one arm extended toward the prize bull from the Orekhov collective, caught mid-gesture. There had been a flood in the storage room six years ago, and the photograph had a water stain across the upper third that had been there since then. What was visible now was Pavel's posture, the way he held his arms — that particular habit of holding them slightly out from his sides, some balance he'd had, like a man always prepared to catch something — and the slope of his shoulders, but not his expression. Alexei had been carrying the photograph for seven years and had stopped looking at it.

He had met Nadezhda Ilyinichna Kuznetsova in Marchuk's waiting room that morning — the regional agricultural economist, in from the city to present findings from the district yield survey. She had asked about the Yablonovka grain storage numbers, then asked a follow-up question before he had finished answering. The district accounts were unusually consistent, she said — had he considered writing up the methodology? He said the methodology was standard. She pressed her card into his hand and said standard methodology consistently applied was rarer than it should be. She was getting off at Verkhne-Sokolova to review the cooperative there, then driving on to the grain depot at Yablonovka in the afternoon — "I'll see you around the depot, perhaps" — and boarded the train.

He had not heard of anyone suggesting he write up his methodology before.

"They audited the Prigorsk accounts office," said Vasya, the conductor, stopping in the aisle without quite stopping, a thing he did when he had information to pass. "Found things. Discrepancies."

Alexei asked what kind.

"Several kinds." Vasya moved on.

Alexei thought about the quarterly folder now in Marchuk's pile, and thought about the word discrepancies, and thought about the fact that his totals agreed across three recalculations. His left hand pressed down on the folder without his intending it. None of this had helped since Voronov, the new committee chair, had started attending the quarterly reviews.

He thought about Pavel. The photograph in the briefcase. He could see Pavel's posture with complete clarity — the arms, the slope, the mid-gesture — but when he tried to see the face behind the water stain it would not come. Seven years since the granary accident. A face he had known for twenty-six years, and now what he had was posture and arms.

At Verkhne-Sokolova, Kuznetsova got off. He watched through the window: she moved through the small crowd on the platform, said something to a man waiting with a clipboard, took the clipboard, looked at the top sheet, handed it back. She did not look back at the train. He watched her until she was gone.

The station café at Verkhne-Sokolova: the smell of black tea and something fried in old fat; two truck drivers at the counter who laughed at the same point in every sentence; a long-haired dog sleeping under the pastry shelf, its ribs visible but slow. Alexei bought tea and stood at the window. He took Kuznetsova's card from his breast pocket and looked at it, then put it away.

"The Yablonovka accountant," he heard the woman behind the counter say to the driver beside her. "He sorted out the Selivanov collective last winter — they were going to lose the spring subsidy, he came out himself. Stayed until it was right."

Alexei turned his cup in his hands and looked at the dog.

Back on the train, moving again, Vasya said the Yablonevo grain collective — the one east, not Yablonovka — had sold to investors from the city. Solar installation. He stopped this time, turned slightly, waited. Alexei had seen the Yablonevo figures in a district accounts review the previous autumn — the per-hectare settlement had been fourteen percent below the cooperative's five-year yield average, a number that meant the timing had not been the collective's to choose. He said: I heard that too. Vasya nodded with the mild satisfaction of a man who has expected it for years and moved on.

Alexei thought about the Yablonovka grain depot, where Kuznetsova was headed by road, where the third-quarter submission would eventually need to go. He thought about what investors from the city did with agricultural land after they acquired it. He arrived at the quarterly folder again.

Kuznetsova's card. He took it out and held it. The print was the standard type used by the regional economics office: name, title, address, office number. She had asked a follow-up question before he had finished answering. On the back, nothing.

Kuznetsova's card. Marchuk had given him the same guidance document twice. He woke at four most mornings and lay rechecking the figures — not because he feared error, but his mind had arranged itself around the preparation for error in a way he could no longer switch off. His shoulders had changed; he held them forward when he stood, as if presenting himself for inspection. His handwriting had gotten smaller over the years, a millimeter or two per year, measured once against an old letter. He was fifty-five. He put the card in his pocket.

At a station stop he did not know the name of, a clerk in a worn jacket was working his way along the platform with a clipboard, tapping two fingers against the side of each car, marking something, moving on. A woman waiting on the platform said something to him; he shook his head without looking at her and kept going. When he reached the end of the platform, he turned and walked back the way he had come.

The train slowed for the low section before Yablonovka. Construction on the left. Ahead: the grain depot elevator, the cluster of rooftops, the water tower.

The road crossing before Yablonovka: the barrier down. A car waiting on the other side of the tracks. And inside the car — he could see through the train windows, two cars' worth of sightline — Kuznetsova in the passenger seat, looking at something in her lap, her profile clear in the late afternoon light. She had driven from Verkhne-Sokolova. The road was faster than the train between the two towns; she had said she would be at the depot. Of course. The barrier was between them. The folder on his knees. She looked up as the train passed, and Alexei, without deciding, lifted two fingers from the folder in something that was not quite a wave.

And beside the road on the near side of the crossing: a boy of perhaps seventeen, waiting at the verge, standing with his arms slightly out from his body — that particular stance, that balance-habit — back to the train, watching the car. As the train moved past the crossing, the boy turned to look after it, and Alexei caught the angle of his face for a moment through the glass: open, attentive, wholly present, not yet managing itself. And Alexei's chest understood before his mind did, and then his mind did too, and what came back was a specific afternoon and not a general impression: the district agricultural fair, a Saturday in August, the smell of animals and cut straw and dust, Pavel's voice saying something — a specific sentence, "Papa, come look at this one, it won't stand still for anyone else" — the heat, the particular light, Pavel's face turned toward him, the expression fully present: pleased with a private thing, wanting to share it. The weight of his son's hand on his arm. Complete.

He pressed his hands flat on the folder.

Kuznetsova's car on the other side of the barrier, still visible. Pavel was thirty-three and argued with him every August about the livestock at the Orekhov stand, the same argument they had never settled. The methodology had been written; someone in the city was reading it.

The barrier lifted.

The Voronov committee's third-quarter formatting requirements. Here was Yablonovka. Here he was.
