My brother sold his hockey gear to a pawn shop. My mom told me that while I was looking for the Nintendo 64 we'd gotten one Christmas, which came with Wayne Gretzky's 3D Hockey and two controllers. She sighed and said he’d probably sold that too.

I told myself I was done with hockey, but I couldn't get rid of my gear the way he had. My addiction was alcohol, and I could afford it, so I stole it away for nearly twenty years, just like my Easton bag. If it was hidden, it was fine.

Growing up in Rockford, Illinois, we learned to skate before we could read. My whole family played—Dad in an adult C league, Mom one of the founders of the multi-age Rockford Hot Wings. My brother made the Rockford Icemen as a freshman and won state championships his junior and senior years, starting what would become fifteen consecutive state titles for the program. Five years younger, I followed with three.

We played every single day. If there wasn't a game, there was practice. If there wasn't practice, there was dryland. If there wasn't dryland, we shot at a tarp Dad hung in the garage. In the summer, street hockey. In the winter, pond hockey. At a restaurant, table hockey. In a hallway, shinny hockey. Hockey was a way of life, a scaffolding that held us up. We slept because there was always a morning game. We ate because we'd be on the ice in three hours. We kept our feet moving and our heads up.

If you’re not familiar, hockey players often minimize. I had a broken collarbone, two concussions, and my right hip makes me limp after a distance. My first concussion, my vision went bright yellow with sparkles. I don’t remember the second one. I still feel like I got off easy. At my brother’s game once, I watched a dad tape up his son's dislocated shoulder on the bench and send him back out. Another time, a parent opened the bench door and yanked a kid's helmet off while he was pounding on a downed player, to even the odds. The police came and tear-gassed the rink.

After graduating, about half our team went to college and the rest went into construction, the military, or firefighting. When I left for college, the structure evaporated. I kept drinking. Hockey shrank to a bag of equipment I buried among other things. Years passed that way. Graduate school. A decade on the West Coast. Eventually I moved to Madison, and one afternoon, tired of hiding, I drug out the Easton bag and sanded rust off my skate blades. At Play-It-Again Sports on Odana Road, the guy behind the counter turned one of them over in his hand.

"You sure you want to do this?" he said. "I can't guarantee the blade will hold if I put it in the machine."

"It's fine."

At the Madison Ice Arena I stood behind the glass watching the top-tier league warm up—former Badgers, ex-semipros from the Capitols, and skaters who'd played juniors. One, I later learned, was the emergency goalie for the Rockford IceHogs, my hometown's AHL team.

A group of players stood nearby, so I walked up and asked about ice time. It felt like finding an AA meeting, or a veterans bar: Nobody needed explanations.

"Sunday nights there's the OBHL—Old Buzzard Hockey League—in Sun Prairie. Teams are picked by lottery. Thirty-five-and-over in Verona, but you need to know someone. Andrea might know somebody. Don't get too drunk though—there’s a great morning skate in Sun Prairie, five-thirty.

Thirty-and-over here at MIA, but it’s fast and a bit chippy. Three-on-three on the studio rink. Pickups Tuesday and Thursday in McFarland. DeForest, Oregon, Waunakee, Middleton—”

As he continued, it occurred to me that hockey was still a structure after all.

"The morning one,” I interjected. “Did you say five-thirty?"

It's 10:15 p.m. on a Sunday night at the Verona Ice Arena, and I'm heading for the bench. A defenseman pinched, the puck was chipped out, and suddenly it's three-on-one the other way. Time to sit down. "Nice backcheck, buddy," someone chirps. "That's a veteran change right there." I wheeze and grab my water bottle. "I'm forty," I say between breaths. "I wouldn't catch up anyway." We watch the rush develop. The guy with the puck refuses to pass and fans on the shot. We bang on the boards. "Nice swing, buddy!" This is the thirty-five-and-over summer league in Verona. Our team is Light Blue. We typically lose. I also drive out to Stoughton's Mandt Center to play roller with Madison Rec Hockey, and lose there too. I love it here. Now I better go to bed. There’s an early pickup tomorrow. These days there’s always somewhere to be.
