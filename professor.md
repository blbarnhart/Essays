# The Professor

He was bent over Roger Penrose's The Road to Reality, a tome of one thousand pages spread open to some middle chapter, like ancient script of latin, on the desk before him. It was a simple office, with chalkboard along the longest wall, and the desk abutted to the corner near the window. Shelves overladen with papers and books in obscene piles. He sat huddled over his oak desk and copied an equation into a green notebook with a pencil worn down to the ferrule. His cardigan was buttoned to the collar, each button set, the hem level. Thinning gray hair combed neatly from left to right. When I knocked, he lifted his head, startled, and I’d have there was something in the look of his eyes that resembled fear. Cold, innocent, childish fear -- him staring up at me and me staring down at him -- before he grimaced it away. 

I had come from the graduate office down the hall, where the students were packing away the last of their belongings. Exams were over, the semester finally come to a close, so they were finally at ease. They shared imitations of teachers and guessed who they might be. One crossed the room in a bent shuffle, a hand trembling out in front of him, saying, "If we start with an orthogonal subspace," in the professor's stretched, careful voice. The others laughed. 

“Too easy!” 

As sad as it was, it reminded me, and I slipped out then, hurried down the long corridor to the line of professor’s offices, and in a hushed, reverent voice, told him, in the doorway of his office while he sat at his desk, “Your course was my favorite."

He winced. “Really?" he said. “I thought all you wanted was good marks.”

My shoulders tightened, and I cursed myself for coming – how could it be this hard to relay good news? 

I’m trying to brighten this man, not perform another examination. I thought immediately to graduate students down the hall, and their irreverence to his intellect. The graciousness he gave to us, in patient. We owed this man. And here I was the one to do it, only to be met with pushback. But he had only expressed surprise. Perhaps again I was being too hard on others.

“It would’ve been nice to examine some of the more nuanced versions of quantum electromagnetic theory.” 


"I'm sorry," I said. "I just wanted to say goodbye."

I shifted my bag higher on my shoulder, and he lowered his eyes back to the book before him. 
  
"Goodbye, then," he said.

That was the last time I saw him at the university, or anywhere for that matter. The next day, his office and the entire school was locked, and he sojourned away with the rest of the group. The next semester, the books were gone from the shelves, his brown mug from the desk, and all the piles of papers. Later I heard he had sold his house, given away his furniture, and emptied his bank account in a series of small withdrawals. Someone said he had drowned himself in the river. Someone else said he had gone to India with a charity group. The department chair said only that Professor Emil Varro had retired.

The evening after I knocked on his office door, he finished the line he had been copying, closed the green notebook, and placed it squarely on top of Penrose’s tome. He wiped the desk with a dry cloth, tidying the last of his things, and went home. 

At home he did not turn on the hallway light. The house stood narrow between two wider houses, its brick front darkened by rain. He hung his coat on the second hook, placed his keys in a shallow dish, and boiled water for tea. He did not make dinner. He set one cup on the table, measured tea leaves into the strainer, then added bergamot from a small tin whose lid had lost its shine.

Two crackers went on a white plate, parallel, edges aligned.

He ate standing beside the table. Afterward he rinsed the cup, dried it with a towel folded over the oven handle, and returned it to the left side of the second shelf. He washed the plate and set it upright in the rack, though there was no other dish to lean against.

In the bedroom, he turned his watch face down on the bedside table. Beside it, he placed a small photograph face down. He folded his glasses and set them next to the photograph. Then he smoothed the blue-and-white duvet twice with the flats of his hands, got under the covers, lay on his back, and took three measured breaths.

In the morning, his eyes opened to the window frame beyond his feet. Sunlight, caught in the lower pane, made a small orange bead on the wall. He reached for the bedside table, passed over the watch and the photograph, and took his glasses.

The blue notebook was for mornings. It lay at the center of the kitchen table. One problem only. He sharpened his pencil over the wastebasket, tapped the shavings from the blade, and began. He worked until the problem yielded a solution, a boundary, or until the light from the kitchen window touched the far cabinet.

That morning the proof from the night before settled without resistance. By ten, he closed the notebook, ran one finger along the spine, and set it beside the sugar bowl he never used.

He folded his towel into thirds, placed his goggles in a small biscuit tin, aligned his rubber sandals by the back door, and went to the pool.

The route never varied. Two blocks past the bakery, left at the church with the loose gutter, through the alley behind the dry cleaner. The pool building smelled of chlorine and wet concrete. In the corridor before the locker room, each step answered him from the tiled wall.

At the near lane, the coach was leaning over a swimmer, his whistle swinging from his neck.

"Eighteen years," the coach said, nodding at the professor as he passed. "Not one morning missed. Not one."

The swimmer looked up, water running from his cap into his eyebrows.

Professor Varro stepped into the lane, settled his goggles over his eyes, and pushed off. His arms moved without splash. He counted each length behind his teeth. At thirty-two he stopped, climbed out, and dried between his toes before putting on his sandals.

The red notebook was for afternoons. A second problem, related but distinct. He opened it after tea. At six, he closed it. At six fifteen, he boiled a potato, sliced it into a bowl, added salt, and ate at the table. He washed the bowl immediately, dried it, and returned it to its place.

At night he read until the room had darkened completely around the page. Then the watch went face down. The photograph beside it. The glasses folded. Two passes over the duvet. Three breaths.

The next morning he passed over the watch and the photograph, took his glasses, filled the kettle to the same mark, measured tea and bergamot, and placed two crackers in parallel on the plate.

The knock came when he had nearly reached the end of the morning's problem.

Two taps. A pause. One more.

He did not move. His pencil rested above the page, the point touching nothing. The knock came again, the same broken rhythm.

He closed the blue notebook. The pencil rolled toward the edge; he caught it before it fell and placed it lengthwise across the cover.

A girl stood on the step with a notebook clamped against her chest. She wore a green school coat with one sleeve darker than the other from rain. Her hair had been pulled back with a red band, but half of it had loosened and stuck to her cheek. She looked not at his face but at the buttons of his shirt.

"I have a problem," she said.

He looked past her to the street. No one waited at the gate. A bicycle leaned against the opposite curb, its front wheel turned.

"I was retired," he said.

She nodded once. "It's only one."

Rainwater had gathered in the grooves of her shoes. A drop fell from the hem of her coat onto his threshold.

He opened the door wider.

"Ten minutes," he said.

She stepped inside and did not wipe her feet until he pointed to the mat.

At the kitchen table she placed her notebook beside his blue one. Its cover was bent and soft at the corners. She opened to a page covered in forward-leaning symbols and arrows she had drawn too heavily. Some had torn through the paper.

He sat across from her, drew the notebook nearer, and read.

The problem was elementary. But there, in the third line, was a turn that was not merely wrong. It was ambitious. She had tried to cross a gap by naming it a bridge.

"This step," he said, tapping the page.

She leaned forward.

"You cannot divide by an expression before you know whether it vanishes."

"But if it vanishes, the answer is already there."

"No," he said. "Then a different door opens."

She looked at the page as if it might have changed. Her fingers tightened on the cuff of her coat.

He wrote two cases in the margin. His handwriting was small and upright, each symbol separated from the next. She watched the pencil, not his face.

When the ten minutes passed, the problem had been separated into its parts and solved. He closed her notebook and returned it.

She stood. "Thank you."

At the door she turned as though to say something else, but a car passed through a puddle at the curb and threw water against the gate. She looked toward it, then left.

He shut the door. In the kitchen, he wiped the wet prints from the floor with a cloth. He returned to the table, opened the blue notebook, and stared at the final line of his own problem. After a minute he erased it. The paper thinned under the rubber. He rewrote the same line with a smaller hand.

The next day, he did as he always did. Watch, photograph, glasses. Kettle to the mark. Tea and bergamot. Two crackers in parallel. Cup rinsed, dried, returned. Blue notebook opened.

Before the second line, he looked toward the door.

Nothing.

He bent to the page.

At the pool, the coach said something about the weather, but Professor Varro had already fixed his goggles. He lost count at nineteen and had to begin again from sixteen. At home, he drank his second tea without crackers and sat by the window with his hands on his knees.

Only in the afternoon, when the room had taken on the thin gray light that came before evening, did the knock sound again.

One tap.

He stood so quickly that the chair legs struck the floor.

The girl held a folded sheet of paper. Her coat was dry today. There was a bruise the size of a thumbprint near her wrist, yellow at the edges, visible where her sleeve had ridden up.

"I tried it your way," she said.

He took the page. She had written carefully, though not neatly. The first half was correct. The error came later, where she had refused a simple substitution and invented a longer path. He followed it to the end and found, under the mistake, the shape of the right question.

"It is not wrong from the beginning," he said.

Her mouth opened a little, then closed. She looked down and rubbed the heel of one shoe over the other.

"How long will this one take?"

He looked behind him. The red notebook lay open on the table. His afternoon problem had been waiting for the next line. Outside, the gutter at the corner dripped in a slow, uneven count.

He should have said ten minutes.

Instead he said, "Come in."




The second intrusion arrives the next day.

The knock comes earlier this time, before he has begun the blue notebook. She enters with less hesitation. He does not set the condition again, but the hour holds. She sits, opens her notebook, and begins to explain what she has tried.

He listens.

This time, before they begin, he prepares tea. Two cups. He places the plate of crackers between them, not aligned as before but set within reach of both.

As they work, she asks more questions. Not only about the steps, but about the reasons behind them. Why must it be this way? What happens if it is not? He answers with the same precision, but finds that her questions do not stop where his answers do. She pushes further, into places where he has to think before he speaks.

She mentions her uncle once, while they are still working. A mathematician, she says. Hungarian. He died some years before she was born — she does not know exactly when. Her mother will not speak of him. She has seen one photograph: a small man in a plain coat, caught mid-movement, as if passing through. He traveled constantly. He owned almost nothing. His name was Paul.

He sets down his pencil. His mouth moves once, then closes. After a moment, he asks how she found this address.

Her mother gave it to her, she says. A man who worked with her uncle. She was told he might be able to help.

He picks up the pencil and returns to the line.

She stops working a few minutes before the end of the hour. She says, without looking up from the page, that she might not be able to come for a few days. There are some things at home. He does not ask.

He does not extend the session. But when she leaves, the table remains set. The second cup is still half full. He finishes it standing at the counter before washing both.

The third intrusion is not a knock.

He expects her at the usual time. The blue notebook is closed. The table is cleared. Two cups are set. The kettle is ready.

She does not come.

He waits. The light shifts. The tea cools. He pours it out, washes the cups, and proceeds with the day.

The next day, he prepares two cups again. The crackers are placed between them. He opens the blue notebook but does not begin. He waits.

She does not come.

The routine continues, but now it contains an absence. The blue notebook is still used, the problem still approached, but before the first line there is a pause, a glance toward the door at intervals. At the pool, he loses his count at the far end and starts again from one.

Days pass.

He begins to prepare an extra plate at dinner. It sits across from him, untouched. He does not remove it until he has finished eating.

In the evening, he finds himself at the window. The street is narrow. Few people pass.

Two cups.

In the mornings now, his hand goes to the blue notebook more slowly. His shoulders do not settle when he sits; he holds them slightly forward, as though still listening for the knock. The crackers on the plate are arranged for one.

Eventually, the structure no longer holds.

One morning, after setting the kettle and arranging the crackers, he does not open the blue notebook. He stands at the window, watches the light take its place, and then steps outside. He has not checked his buttons.

The walk is not fixed.

He begins with the familiar route — past the bakery, the church with the loose gutter — but at the corner he does not turn. He continues into streets he has not mapped. The houses are similar but not identical.

He asks a neighbor — a woman watering plants in front of her house — if she knows the girl. He describes the green coat, the red band. The woman considers, then nods toward a street further down. Another house. A family that moved in recently.

He goes there.

The house is quieter than he expects. He hesitates at the door, then knocks — two taps, a pause, then another. The rhythm is unfamiliar in his own hand.

A man answers. Through the open door, on a shelf near the entrance, a framed photograph: a small man in a plain coat, unsmiling, caught mid-movement.

He knows that face. It is the face on the photograph on his bedside table, the one he has passed over every morning without looking at it since the year Paul died.

A conference room in Budapest. Paul standing at the board writing quickly, his coat still on, talking before he had fully arrived, passing around a small card with a problem written on it — twenty dollars if you solved it by morning, fifty if you didn't stop until morning. The chalk dust in the air. Paul's handwriting: small, tilted, cramped with certainty.

Paul came back in spring and left a problem at the door. The girl came in the afternoon with her notebook against her chest and her symbols leaning forward, and they worked until the room had gone dark.

There is a brief exchange. The girl, he learns, has been staying elsewhere. A change in circumstances. School, or family — not fully explained. She will not be returning.

He listens. He nods. He thanks the man and turns away.

The walk back is slower.

When he returns, the kettle is still on the counter. The crackers remain on the plate. The blue notebook lies open, untouched.

He does not remove the second cup.

He prepares the tea, pours both cups, and sits at the table. The top button of his cardigan has come open, the hem hanging lower on the left.

After a moment, he opens the blue notebook and writes a new problem. It is not drawn from a journal or a memory of past work. It is something simpler, shaped by the questions she had asked — by the places where his previous answers had not been sufficient.

He begins.

He pauses after the second line. Reads it back. Continues.

Outside, the light has reached the far wall.
